Netflix's Global Strategy

Apr 21, 2021

Netflix’s recent international expansion has been aggressive and marked in comparison to its competitors. As streaming platforms from fellow media giants like Disney, HBO, and ViacomCBS swarm the market - taking back their licensed content with them - Netflix is leveraging its broad subscriber base and market dominance to explore a previously untapped global audience and to create a vast library of original content in the process.

The company has been famously opaque about how it quantifies the success of its licensed and originals, but a quick dive into its US-region content library, and how it has changed over the years, can show us what’s has been successful on the platform and signal where both Netflix and the world of streaming could be headed.

Licensed Content

The International Origins of the US Content Library

As an ad-free platform, Netflix relies on maintaining and growing subscriptions as its main source of revenue. With an increasingly saturated domestic market where Netflix has already achieved significant market dominance 1 2, international markets show the most promise for future revenue growth. Since licensing and producing original content is cost-intensive, examining where Netflix has put its money may indicate what its plans are in regards to international expansion.

Looking at the figure above, we can see four distinct categories that the top 10 ‘countries of production’ fit into: English-speaking countries, Western European countries, Spanish-speaking countries, and Asian-Pacific (APAC) countries. Though many factors would go into determining where (ie in what ‘country of production) content is produced, languages and cultural ideas presented in the content could be presumed to be definitive ones, making these regional groupings reasonable delineators.

Looking back at the figure above, we can see an inflection point for internationally-produced content in 2016, which lines up with Netflix’s live announcement of ‘going international’ (“NetflixEverywhere”) on January 6, 2016 at the Consumer Electronics Show. The move expanded the platform to 150 countries with a promise of adding four more languages to the seventeen already supported at the time 3.

Since 2016, the US, UK, and India have been the top locations for Netflix-produced content. This may signal Netflix’s prioritization of English-language titles in both licensed and original content, but it also may just be a reflection of how the US, UK, and India are homes to the largest entertainment industries in the world with sizeable pools of readily available content.

If we look outside the top 10 countries of production, a more obvious pattern emerges.

The highest cumulative content counts can be found in a few distinct countries: the US, India, UK, Canada, France, Japan, Spain, and South Korea. As a whole, these concentrations of numbers mostly lie in ‘affluent’ and ‘converging’ markets as defined by the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) 4. This could be because certain countries are positioned to have booming middle-classes in the upcoming decade and others are already considered affluent with large media-consumer bases. These two economic profiles seem to be where Netflix is honing in on to build a sturdy foundation for its international expansion. Possible other factors contributing to these decisions include countries with large entertainment industries with ready-to-be licensed content, the availability of affordable licensing contracts, and minimal (regulatory, linguistic, and cultural) barriers to entry. But as Netflix’s budget for acquiring and producing global content expands, we may see new prominent ‘regions’ of content emerge from OECD-defined ‘stalled’ economies such as Mexico, Egypt, and Turkey, some of which already produce a significant amount of content that is cross-culturally popular 5.

This being said, looking at the numbers of titles being produced in each country is just one facet of breaking down Netflix’s international streaming strategy. Front and center of any interaction on the Netflix platform is genre – categories of titles that immediately show up during start-up. These genres are specifically catered to users’ viewing habits with the main purpose of helping them make a viewing selection within the first 90 seconds 1. When we consider genre along with the countries of production in the current library, we may be able to see similarities between regions, indicating a deliberate library curation strategy that caters to different audience bases. This not only suggests what Netflix - powered by algorithmically-sourced user data - thinks is the future of international streaming, but what it ultimately may end up being given Netflix’s considerable influence.

Countries of Production and Genre

Expanding to a larger sample with the top 15 countries of production – and keeping in mind the previously defined regional categories (including the new entry of the Middle East in the top 15) – we can see several commonalities of genre between countries within the same region.

Let’s first take a look Netflix’s genre breakdowns for movies. APAC countries, with the exclusion of India and Japan, have the most content in the genres ‘Action-Adventure’ and ‘Comedies’. This is in contrast to having ‘Dramas’ as the most popular genre in the rest of the top 15.

This trend of having the genre ‘Dramas’ being the most dominant share can, however, be seen in India, a large APAC country where ‘Dramas’ and ‘Comedies’ are the first and second largest genre. This is similar to the English-speaking, Spanish-speaking, Western European, and Middle Eastern regions.

Japan-produced content lies largely in ‘Anime’ but follows the APAC trend with ‘Action-Adventure’ closely following.

Middle-Eastern countries share noticeable symmetry with each other, with genres ‘Comedies’ and ‘Dramas’ as their largest stakes.

This symmetry in dominant genres can also be seen with Spanish-speaking countries with ‘Dramas’ and ‘Comedies’, Western Europe with ‘Dramas’, ‘Documentaries’, and ‘Comedies’, and English-speaking countries where the symmetry is split between two groups: 1. where the US & the UK are similar, with large shares of ‘Dramas’, ‘Thrillers’, ‘Documentaries’, and ‘Comedies’ being common, and 2. Australia & Canada who share ‘Children & Family Movies’ and ‘Documentaries’ as their most dominant genres. Though these patterns are clear for movies, it remains to be seen if similar patterns emerge in Netflix’s TV content.

Genre, and the regions of licensed content production it overlaps with, not only indicates what Netflix users from around the world are consuming from those countries, but what audiences from those regions (given that content is oftentimes confined by language and cultural understanding) are demanding.

Genre and Region in TV

Looking at the figures above, genre breakdowns for TV shows seem to follow less of a detectable pattern. ‘TV Comedies‘ share similar, sizeable proportions across notable countries in separate regions: US, Australia, Canada, China, and France. Some symmetry can be seen in the largest genres among two groups of countries: Mexico & Spain (the two Spanish-speaking countries) and France and Canada, which have a French-language commonality. ‘Romantic TV Shows’ are far more dominant in APAC countries, and when removing language-specific genre categories (e.g. ‘Korean TV Shows’), there are clear dominant genres in several countries, including ‘Anime Series’ in Japan and ‘TV Dramas’ in Egypt and Turkey.

These overall symmetries suggest well-defined acquisitions and calculated content curation in regards to genre, language, region, and culture. It again implies regional similarities in audience tastes that have been most likely uncovered by Netflix’s algorithm – a robust finding given that it ignores bucketing users into traditional demographic groups like geography, age, and gender, and instead relies purely on what users watch and rate 1. Not only does this hold promise in determining the future of content streaming, but also in understanding new dimensions in the cultural norms and tastes of audiences around the world.

Content Ratings and Age Groups

If we were to take a quick overview of the last metric available to us – content ratings and age groups of regionally-produced content for an expanded top 25 countries – we can see skewed distributions for most countries, except for the US, UK, Canada, and Australia which have more balanced profiles. This confirms a larger selection of available content for English-speaking Netflix audiences and suggests a longer, more informed period of curation of English-language content, given it is the native language of the platform.

Given these insights into Netflix’s US content library, we can start seeing a pattern in both the domestic and emerging international markets. These findings can be a rich source of data on the streaming habits of millions of international users and may indicate where future investments in streaming may be.

Netflix Originals

Starting in 2013, Netflix has been pouring money into producing its own titles. It has now emerged as a major player in the once-exclusive Hollywood studio space. A critical driver of producing cost-intensive originals is that they will permanently be part of Netflix’s library with no threat of being revoked by competing streaming platforms. Not only does it allow Netflix to increase control of its own content library, but also allows for the data-influenced development of ‘custom’ content that’s tailored to the tastes of its of large user base.

Looking into the slice of ‘Netflix originals’ available in the US content library, we can see a few similarities to the library as a whole. Originals content production is also concentrated in similar countries of production (i.e. the US, India, UK, Canada, France, Japan, Spain, and South Korea), where film and TV has always been produced.

The ratings/age group metrics by country are similarly skewed to the US library’s, with the exception that there are no countries that have a balanced profile for all age groups with originals, suggesting at the early stages of content production that has not yet settled into balanced offerings for its users.

Differences between Netflix originals and the whole of the US library really emerge when looking at the breakdown of title counts by type (movies vs. TV shows), duration of titles by type, and genre by country of production and type. Let’s first look at the differences of titles counts for movies and TV between the entire content library and the originals.

We can see that Netflix has heavily invested in producing TV shows over movies for its originals. This could be for many reasons, including a focus on growing revenue which is driven by continued subscriptions, something that longer-running formats like TV shows and miniseries can offer with longer duration and subsequent season releases. They can also create ‘hype’ - something respondents in a recent poll noted as a big incentive for subscribing to multiple streaming platforms 5 and something that is commonly associated with the communal TV viewing/binging experiences. This ‘hype’ has recently been seen for viral Netflix TV shows like Bridgerton, Tiger King, and The Queen’s Gambit. Shorter-duration movie offerings like Extraction and Birdbox have seemingly less cultural influence or possibility of user retention. It remains to be seen if investing in movies can bring similar numbers to the streaming platform, but early investment patterns have shown that for Netflix, TV is king.

Movies

Above are the genre breakdowns for Netflix original movies have been narrowed down to the top 12 countries of production to adjust for the smaller number of titles.

Since Netflix movie originals are a small group at 35 titles, there are no broad or visible patterns in genre and/or region. But what is apparent is that ‘Stand-Up Comedy’ is the largest and most dominant genre for movies in general, which could be because stand-up comedy specials have relatively low production costs and may be easy vehicles of entry into international markets, both of which make them attractive options for Netflix. This investment into stand-up comedy specials could also explain why the most frequent duration for movies dropped from 90-100 min. to 60-70 min. when comparing the licensed US library and Netflix originals. Notably, the most frequent ’length’ of TV shows remained the same between the two groups at 1 season.

Genre in Netflix Originals

Now looking at the genre breakdowns for Netflix original TV shows (again with the top 12 countries of production), we can see a clear diversity of genre within TV originals with total 127 titles to date.

Western European countries – France, Germany, and Italy – share some symmetry for their top genres. This can also be seen in the Spanish-speaking countries of Mexico and Spain.

As for overall genre diversity, the US and UK remain at the top with India, an extremely promising emerging market with a large entertainment industry. Though these patterns are based on a nascent originals library, they echo the findings of the larger licensed US content library that suggest a strategic approach to creating content based on linguistically-/culturally-similar regions.

Is the future strategy of streaming to focus on Kids’ TV in Canada, Australia, and China? Are Dramas the prime investment in Western Europe? Are British TV shows the universally popular export? Powered by Netflix’s algorithm and data from its 200+ million large user base, this user-generated data is a goldmine for examining the emergent streaming ecosystem.

What It Means for the World of Streaming

Netflix’s international expansion has the potential to herald change in the way the entire world consumes media, not only with their own platform, but with the concept of on-demand internationalized content available to everyone around the world simultaneously. With the data above we get a sense of what genres of content are popular in certain regions, whose producing them, and how Netflix and other streaming platforms may develop content tailored to those tastes. Along with regional specialization of genre, we can expect global access to originally-produced content to allow certain genres to seep across international lines, making previously local genres global behemoths. We can also expect the profile of TV to rise as the simple result of Netflix’s revenue model to capture and retain subscribers. Serialized media may take over the draw and prestige of movies, and the diffusion of different genres and culture across the globe can change not only media content but form. All of this, motivated by Netflix’s business goals and algorithm, points to rapid changes in the media landscape we’ve always known.

And these changes are not just confined to media, but affect globalization as a whole. As consumers are increasingly exposed to other cultures on ubiquitous platforms like Netflix, the nature of business and globalization is bound to change in unforeseeable ways. Netflix’s international expansion may not only be a force in globalization and democratization of media, but in the globalization and democratization of culture and language as well.
