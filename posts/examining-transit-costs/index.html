Examining the Hidden Variables and Costs of Transit Projects

May 06, 2026

The conversation around public transit is going through a modern renaissance. With the proliferation of social media and the unprecedented insight given into the daily lives of people around the world, there has been a surge in discussion and activity around the vitality of public transit and why its development has almost slowed down to a halt in America. The problem of building public transit - including the significant costs, red tape, and the considerable public and political will it often involves - has plagued American cities since public transit was displaced by car and highway infrastructure in national politics[1]. As the focus moved from urban centers to suburban sprawl, American political and public interest turned away from developing walkable and interconnected cities to car-centric environments that have become challenging landscapes to integrate or retrofit mass transit.

Along with examining the benefits of transit in other countries, concerns of carbonization and the effects of climate change have added to the call to revisit transit development. But beyond its role as a public good, there has also been discussion around the economic potential left behind with insufficient transit networks, including the large section of Americans who have limited employment options tied to their ability to commute to the workplace and the untapped business potential along corridors where connectivity is lacking[2]. This is all in addition to the more palpable, commonplace dissatisfaction that more and more Americans are feeling – that they would like to move further away from the absolute car dependency that has become central to American life.

The Transit Costs Project

What makes transit costs so high in the US?

To answer this multi-faceted, and oftentimes highly-political, question The Transit Costs Project at NYU’s Marron Institute has embarked on analyzing transit projects (specifically rapid transit, as defined as electric high-capacity transportation built in urban areas, more commonly known as metros) across the world over the last couple of decades to find what low-cost, transit-friendly countries are doing differently than perennially high-cost countries. During their 5-year long project, researchers have compiled case studies such as New York’s Second Avenue Subway Extension and Sweden’s Citybanan, along with supporting data and extensive interviews with project administrators, in order to unravel the complexities of the cost equation. This in-depth investigation has led to a defined set of broad categories that set high-cost transit countries like the US, Canada, and Hong Kong apart from low-cost countries like Sweden, Turkey, and Italy. A summary of their findings can be found in the Executive Summary, which this analysis investigates.

This meta-examination of the Transit Costs Project’s data aims to provide additional dimension to some of points brought up in project, and occasionally, deviate or question some conclusions presented in the pursuit of providing more nuances in the revitalized, multi-coalitionary effort to improve public transit in the US.

High- and Low-Cost Countries

Looking at the Transit Costs Project’s international projects data, we can see some changes from the first publication of the report in 2023. Unweighted and updated for the rate of inflation in 2025, we see a small shift in the top high-cost countries: (in order from highest to lowest) The US, New Zealand, Qatar, Hong Kong, Singapore, and the UK. Medium-cost countries include Germany, Brazil, and Japan. And the low-cost countries of Sweden, Turkey, and Italy are included as guiding case-studies in the research. Other low-cost countries include Bahrain, Portugal, and South Korea. What is notable, by the project authors as well, is that low- and medium-cost countries span multiple regions and political systems, but high-cost countries are mostly concentrated in the Anglosphere (as defined as English-speaking countries) or countries that were in some way in the Anglosphere, like Hong Kong. A possible explanation to this commonality is a prevailing system adopted across the Anglosphere that raises costs - a system the Transit Costs Project’s research suggests is defined by six distinct themes that differentiate high-cost countries from low-cost countries:

  • Politics and decision making
  • Civil service and internal capacity
  • Procurement and risk allocation
  • Utilities and agency coordination
  • Labor
  • Cost-effective design

Politics and decision-making concerns how much micromanaging is allowed by political figures, oftentimes extending timelines and budgets via election cycles, political campaigning for and against transit lines, and higher reliance on elected officials rather than project officials to shepherd projects to completion.

Civil service and internal capacity refers to capable personnel in agencies and governmental bodies that have the (often decades-long) expertise to handle large and complex megaprojects with oversight that ensures that every step during development is optimized to reduce absolute costs and cost overruns. This internal capacity includes contractors, sub-contractors, consultants, and other personnel that oversee a project’s journey from start to finish.

Procurement and risk allocation addresses the way contracts are developed and awarded to design and construction companies through a project’s life cycle. Possible changes in the procurement process include normalizing upfront cost transparency in bids to avoid unannounced cost increases and agencies using a technical score to award contracts on aptitude rather than exclusively on the lowest bid. Risk allocation tackles the issue of privatizing risk, which creates a system of contractors factoring in considerable risk on their end, inflating project costs astronomically in order to err on the side of caution.

Utilities and agency coordination concerns the inevitable task of coordinating and relocating utilities as construction progresses throughout large tracts of land in the city, particularly sections of track that are tunneled. A lack of agency bookkeeping, confusing or overlapping areas of jurisdiction, and lack of governmental oversight for coordination when conflicts arise often results in the managing agency (like the MTA) paying high out-of-pocket expenses to resolve intergovernmental issues and skirmishes in high-cost countries.

Another area, labor, constitutes about 40%-60% of project costs in the American Northeast and only 40%-60% in the low-cost countries of Italy, Turkey, and Sweden. Even in the highest-wage country, Sweden, only 23% of the costs are labor costs. The Transit Costs Project report points to various factors, including overstaffing projects and overtime laws, as leading to avoidable timeline expansions and the ballooning labor-cost factor in the US.

Cost-effective design refers to the overall design of transit projects, including transit lines, rolling stock, and stations - all of which are optimized, standardized, and cost-conscious. Whether it’s the track level (i.e. tunneled, at-grade/street-level, or elevated) or the size and number of stations, design cost-effectiveness largely relies on an inter-agency coordinated effort which, perhaps due to a lack of long-term expertise and project frequency, could be difficult to manage in the US.

This analysis, which looks at the Transit Costs Project’s international project database, provides additional insight into two of the six categories discussed above: civil service and internal capacity, and cost-effective design.

Civil Service and Internal Capacity

Decades of experience is key to creating a pool of in-house expertise. Case-by-case analysis has shown that unlike the US, countries like Turkey and Italy have cultivated a rail-construction ecosystem over decades of developing an integrated network of contractors, regional construction firms, and agencies with accumulated expertise.

By examining high-, medium-, and low-cost countries’ project timelines, we can see the pattern of density and duration of projects for each category post 2000. It gives us a small glimpse into the ‘ramp’ of transit development that may necessitate, and therefore cultivate, a compounding pool of expertise.

From a sampling of 200 projects, we can see the density of projects spanning from 1990 to 2040. The earliest start times for projects in each cost category are similar, around 1990-1995. But there is a larger density of projects concentrated in the low-cost category overall, signaling that a larger number of projects may reduce costs. This is further supported by charting the number of projects per country against their average cost per km.

A best fit regression line shows a downward trend for cost as the number of projects increases. This adds a finer point to the idea presented in the Transit Costs Project’s report that it’s not just the decades of experience that matters, but the number of total projects. This could indicate that a larger number of overall, and especially concurrent, projects generates a collective of specialists through several possible mechanisms:

  • A common professional resource pool where repeated, quick exposure to multiple, fairly contemporaneous projects allows professionals to develop experience that minimizes timeline extensions and cost overruns. This could further explain the Transit Costs Project’s example of governmental agencies in certain countries having more ‘practice’, and thus streamlining all stages of the development process to optimize for efficiency and cost - like Turkey and Italy, who (looking at the bottom right-quadrant of the graph above) have both high project counts and low costs.
  • Multiple active projects leading to cost-effective design through quick experience and standardization
  • Multiple active projects leading to streamlined procurement and risk processes that benefit both agencies and contractors, and also have become standardized
  • Agency and utilities preparedness in coordination, resulting from the need to facilitate the volume of ongoing projects

There is a possibility of a converse association where lower overall costs allow for more project development. Most likely, the association is a mutual feedback loop where lower costs allow for more projects, and more projects allow for lower costs.

Cost-Effective Design

What happens during the design phase of a project can have a multiplicative effect on project costs in both obvious and unforeseen ways. The Transit Costs Project report points to several components including station number, station size/volume, track level, and corridor length.

Tunneled track is commonly considered the most expensive design option out of the three (tunneled, at-grade, and elevated). Though tunnel boring, relocating utilities, construction management, and agency coordination are cost-intensive for discernable reasons, it seems like at-grade construction may surprisingly outpace tunneling costs.

Projects can have tracks that are a mix of all three levels. Looking at the above, projects with higher percentages of at-grade track are positively correlated with cost. This does not seem to be true for tunneled and elevated track. Although counter-intuitive, at-grade tracks oftentimes require expensive land acquisitions, costly above-ground demolitions and restructuring, and can result in political “tug-of-warring” that raise costs and extend timelines.

Platform length similarly has a counter-intuitive effect on costs. Though the report suggests that platform length is positively correlated with costs, there seems to be a parabolic relationship between the two. This could be because shorter platform lengths still have the upstart costs of building a station and accessories around the platform without the cost-benefit of building a certain length of platform. This is different than average platform lengths that balance the cost of stations with (longer) platform lengths that are often needed for longer track projects meant for higher capacity ridership. They also don’t involve the obvious cost drivers of building extremely long platforms, back offices, and larger stations to house them. This middle ground can also be the result of careful design in countries that have figured out the best way to balance between accommodating larger ridership capacity, length of track needed, and platform/station costs.

Disentangling Location, Operations, and Costs

We can take a closer look at costs across countries by looking at how costs vary within countries by city. Looking at the standard deviation of the costs of various projects in different localities in each country, there is an interesting pattern in the spread in both high- and low-cost countries.

The spread in low-cost countries (between cities) is fairly uniform, whereas high-cost countries have larger spreads where certain localities are “pulling up the average”. For example, Miami and Chicago have costs on par with the lowest-cost city of Malmo in the low-cost country of Sweden. But it’s the extremely high-cost cities of New York and Los Angeles that bring the US’ average cost to the top of the table. On the other hand, a country like Turkey has a far more uniform cost per km across cities, resulting in a standard deviation of 54.73 ($MM). The US’ standard deviation of costs per km is 864.23 ($MM).

This suggests a potential avenue to dig deeper - the particular cost drivers of those cities/regions that are separate from drivers that are common to the country as a whole. Similarly, low-cost projects in high-cost countries, like Miami and Chicago, can be examined to understand what can be done to optimize costs in traditionally high-cost countries. Similarly to the US, the highest and lowest cost cities can be examined in countries like Australia, Canada, and Brazil where the standard deviation of cost per km between cities is high. A close investigation can isolate other prolific cost drivers when larger “factors” like federal politics and decision-making are held constant.

These cost factors mostly propagate two main vectors of this analysis: cost overruns and timeline extensions. Given the sensitivity of keeping timelines contained, it’s safe to say that duration would be highly correlated with costs – but the strength of the correlation is sharp and only further highlights the need to manage factors and bring reform to the politics and decision-making, utilities and agency coordination, civil service and internal capacity, and cost-effective design choices of projects to bring to fruition the enthusiasm and commitment found in the conversation around public transit.

The Future of Transit

In addition to the various and multi-faceted solutions presented in the Transit Costs Project reports, a slightly more in-depth look into the true cost of design choices like a track’s grade level or station platform length can highlight more possibilities in the complex problem of modern public transit. Similarly a closer look into the causal effect of having more projects leading to lower costs – whether through lower labor costs, accumulating expertise, or agency optimization – and how it can become a powerful public and political campaign tool for pushing forward popular initiatives instead of allowing the cost of underactivity to become a reason to oppose them. And lastly to learn whether high-cost countries are truly “high-cost” or if there are hidden pockets of efficient planning, design, and expertise that countries on the whole can learn from.

With so much systemic reform needed, it would seem that a wide-scale collective push is required to change the way transit is built in countries where the costs are high and progress has stalled. The challenges ahead do not dissuade this notion, especially in the face of rising demand for more and better public transit, rapid urbanization, and pressing concerns about climate change and decarbonization. But there’s promise in studies underscoring smaller changes and details that can help us conquer the seemingly impossible problem of public transit. With this, we can see that modest, actionable solutions and collective effort hold the key to the future of transit.
